# The Solo Founder's End-to-End Sales Workflow
## Collections Automation SaaS — Pre-Launch, <$200/mo Budget, Start in 7 Days

---

## How to use this document

You're pre-launch, solo, and kick off in 7 days. This playbook covers the full path from "person has never heard of you" to "signed pilot agreement" — the things you actually say, send, click, and track.

Read sections 0–3 this week. Execute section 4 (7-day prep) starting now. Sections 5–12 are your reference library once outreach goes live. Section 13 is the week-by-week plan for days 1–90.

The 2024–2026 data says unambiguously that (a) cold email still works for B2B SaaS but deliverability is now the game, (b) tight ICP beats volume every time, (c) paid pilots outperform free trials for pre-launch sales, and (d) first 10 customers come from direct, personal outreach for almost every SaaS founder story on record. This playbook is built around those four facts.

## 0. Three things that change how you should sell

Most "SaaS sales playbooks" online are written for funded companies with a product, SDR team, and $25k+ ACVs. You have none of those. Three defaults change for your situation:

**Default 1 — Sell outcomes, not software.** You don't have a product to demo yet. You have a problem you understand and a plan to solve it. That's actually an advantage. You're selling "I will help you collect 15% more of your overdue invoices in 30 days" — not "here's my dashboard." Every conversation is a pilot-services conversation wrapped in a software shell.

**Default 2 — Paid pilots, not free trials.** Free trials for pre-launch companies attract tire-kickers and generate false signal. $49–$99 paid pilots with hands-on onboarding filter for real intent, teach you the product to build, and convert 60–90% to ongoing customers when structured correctly (per multiple B2B pilot studies). SaaStr and Headway founders consistently advise against PLG / free trials for new B2B products.

**Default 3 — One vertical, one workflow, one promise.** The incumbents (HighRadius, Tesorio, Gaviti) sell horizontal AR automation to everyone. You cannot beat that. You can beat a construction subcontractor's spreadsheet at chasing invoices with retainage + lien waiver tracking. Pick one. The whole sales workflow — ICP, copy, discovery questions, demo, pricing, onboarding — derives from this choice.

If these three don't feel right to you, stop and figure out why before spending a dollar. The rest of the playbook assumes you agree.

## 1. Pick your wedge vertical (do this in the next 48 hours)

Four verticals are worth considering. Score them honestly. Pick one.

| Vertical | DSO pain | Tech stack | Decision maker | Access | Sales cycle | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Construction subcontractors** | 60–120 days, retainage, lien waivers | QuickBooks / Sage / Foundation | Owner or Controller | State license DBs, AGC/ASA dirs | Medium (4–8 wks) | **Best default.** Acute pain, accessible lists, unserved by incumbents. |
| **Staffing / recruiting agencies** | Net 45–90, payroll funding gap | QuickBooks / Bullhorn / Avionte | Owner/CFO | ASA, SIA, LinkedIn | Fast (3–6 wks) | Strong second. Cash flow panic = urgency. |
| **Healthcare practices** (non-insurance AR) | 45–70 days, patient balances | QuickBooks / practice management | Practice Manager / Owner | State dirs, specialty assns | Medium | Heavy compliance. Defer unless you have healthcare background. |
| **Professional services** (agencies, consultancies) | ~60 days, many small clients | QuickBooks / Xero / FreshBooks | Owner/Founder | LinkedIn, directories | Fast | Good if you have existing network. Crowded by FreshBooks etc. |

**Recommendation: Construction subcontractors, 10–100 employees, US-based, QuickBooks users.** Reasoning: (1) the pain is the most acute and most specific — retainage and lien-waiver tracking don't exist in generic AR tools, (2) list-building is easy via state contractor license databases, (3) buying power is real (an SMB subcontractor losing 2 weeks of cash flow is an existential problem), and (4) they are underserved — Kolleno, Chaser, Centime all target broader mid-market.

Write this down on one line and tape it above your desk:

> "I help US construction subcontractors with 10–100 employees who use QuickBooks collect overdue invoices and retainage faster, without nagging their GCs."

Everything downstream — list filters, email copy, discovery questions, pricing — comes from that sentence. If you change verticals later, rewrite this line first, then rewrite everything else.

## 3. Seven-day pre-launch checklist (the next 168 hours)

This is non-negotiable. If you skip this and start sending, your domains die inside two weeks and you're stuck.

### Day 1 (tomorrow)
- [ ] Buy 3 secondary domains. Avoid `.com` on your main brand; buy `.co`, `.io`, `.com` variants or lookalikes for outreach (e.g., `tryacme.com`, `get-acme.co`, `acme-io.com`). Keep your primary brand `.com` pristine for transactional mail only.
- [ ] Set up Google Workspace on each of the 3 domains. Create 3 mailboxes per domain (9 total). Real-sounding human names, not "sales@" or "hello@" — `john@`, `jane.smith@`, etc.
- [ ] For EACH domain, configure DNS records:
  - **SPF**: `v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com ~all`
  - **DKIM**: enable in Google Workspace Admin → Apps → Gmail → Authenticate Email (2048-bit key)
  - **DMARC**: `v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:you@primary-domain.com; pct=100`
  - **MX**: Google's standard MX records
- [ ] Set `p=none` on DMARC to start. Move to `p=quarantine` only after you have 30 days of clean data in Google Postmaster Tools. [[source: Google Workspace sender guidelines, 2026](https://support.google.com/a/answer/81126)]

### Day 2
- [ ] Verify SPF/DKIM/DMARC using [mail-tester.com](https://mail-tester.com) and Google Postmaster Tools (https://postmaster.google.com). Every one of your 9 inboxes must pass all three.
- [ ] Set up a simple one-page landing site on your primary brand `.com` (Carrd, Framer, or a static page). Not the outreach domains — those stay bare. Required elements: one-sentence value prop, one proof point (even an industry stat works), embedded Cal.com link.
- [ ] Connect all 9 inboxes to Instantly. Enable warmup on every single one. Set warmup to start at 3–5 emails/day, ramp to ~30/day over 3 weeks.
- [ ] Set up HubSpot CRM Free. Create one deal pipeline with stages: `Sourced → Contacted → Replied → Meeting Booked → Discovery Done → Pilot Proposed → Pilot Active → Won → Lost`.

### Day 3
- [ ] Write your ICP statement (section 1 above) at the top of a working doc. Write your single-vertical promise.
- [ ] Build first 500-row prospect list (instructions in section 5).
- [ ] Verify the list through NeverBounce. Drop anything flagged as "invalid" or "unknown" — keep "valid" and "accept_all".
- [ ] Draft your 5-email sequence (templates in section 6). Don't try to be clever. Use the templates.

### Day 4
- [ ] Build your discovery call script (section 8) and save as a Google Doc you'll open for every call.
- [ ] Build your pilot proposal template (section 11).
- [ ] Build your pricing one-pager (section 10).
- [ ] Record a 3-minute Loom walking through a Figma mockup of your product. Don't over-produce. This is your "pre-launch demo."

### Day 5
- [ ] Stress-test the cold email sequence: send it to 3 friends who aren't your target ICP. Ask them which line made them curious, which made them suspicious, which they'd skip. Iterate.
- [ ] Set up Cal.com with a 30-min "Intro call" link. Buffer 15 min between calls. Integrate with Google Calendar. Block off your no-meeting hours.
- [ ] Join 3 communities (sections 7): r/smallbusiness, r/Construction (if construction vertical), and one targeted Slack like Finance Alliance or a trade-specific forum. Set profile photos. Post once — no links.

### Day 6 (warmup continues — don't skip)
- [ ] Write your first lead magnet: a Google Sheet DSO calculator or AR aging template, gated behind an email capture. Takes an afternoon. It becomes link-able in emails, LinkedIn posts, and community answers.
- [ ] Join LinkedIn. Optimize profile headline: `Helping [vertical] get paid faster | Founder, [brand]`. Post once introducing what you're building (no pitch, no link — just a stat + question).

### Day 7 (Day 0 of outreach)
- [ ] Final check: Instantly warmup is 7 days in and open rates on warmup traffic look healthy (60%+). If not, wait another 7 days — this is the single most important discipline in cold email.
- [ ] Do a final mail-tester.com run from each of your 9 inboxes. Must score 9/10 or higher.
- [ ] Turn on your first campaign at 20 emails per inbox per day (not 50 — ramp slowly). You're now live.

## 4. Day 0–90 tactical plan (also see section 13 for the weekly breakdown)

Three phases, 30 days each. Targets are realistic — not aspirational.

**Phase 1 — Ramp (Days 1–30):** 1,500 prospects contacted, 60–75 positive replies, 15–25 discovery calls booked, 3–5 pilots in progress. Heavy list-building and copy iteration. Zero paying customers is fine; what you're buying is signal.

**Phase 2 — Convert (Days 31–60):** Continue at 1,500–2,500 prospects/month. 5–10 pilot customers active. First 2–3 converting from pilot → paid. MRR crosses $500.

**Phase 3 — Compound (Days 61–90):** First case study published. Referral asks begin. 5–8 paying customers. MRR $1,500–$3,000. One second channel (community or SEO) starts producing inbound.

Solo-founder SaaS benchmarks to calibrate against: Josh Haynam sent 1,200 cold emails to get Interact's first customers. Robi Ganguly sent 25–100 emails per day for months at Apptentive. You should not expect more efficient results than them.

## 5. ICP and target list building

### 5.1 Define the ICP firmographically

Use these filters (Apollo Free supports all of them):

- **Industry**: Construction — specifically NAICS 238x (Specialty Trade Contractors). If targeting a sub-vertical, pick one: electrical (238210), plumbing/HVAC (238220), roofing (238160), etc.
- **Employee count**: 10–100
- **Revenue**: $1M–$25M (most subs)
- **Country**: United States
- **Technology** (Apollo offers tech filters): QuickBooks, Xero, or Sage 100 Contractor visible on their site/stack
- **Titles to target (in order of preference)**:
  1. Owner / Founder / President (for sub-10-employee firms, this is usually who writes the check)
  2. Controller / Finance Manager
  3. CFO
  4. Office Manager (for 10–30 employee firms, this person often runs collections)
  5. A/R Manager / Billing Manager (for 30–100 employee firms)

### 5.2 Build the list in three passes

**Pass 1 — Firmographic base (Apollo Free).** Use the filters above. Export 500 companies to start. Apollo Free gives you 10,000 email credits/month — don't waste them by exporting 5,000 at once before you've seen if the targeting works.

**Pass 2 — Intent enrichment.** Add prospects that are actively signaling the pain:
- Companies with recent job postings for "AR Specialist," "Collections," "Billing Coordinator," "Office Manager" (search Indeed/LinkedIn; a tool like Ashby's free job-scraping or simply Indeed RSS feeds works)
- Companies that recently raised debt/equity (Crunchbase) — cash flow is top of mind
- Companies mentioned in Reddit threads or forums complaining about late payments

**Pass 3 — Local/trade-specific (manual).** Many high-intent subs aren't well-enriched in Apollo. Supplement with:
- State contractor license databases (CSLB in California, TDLR in Texas, etc.) — free public data
- AGC (Associated General Contractors) member directories
- ABC (Associated Builders and Contractors) chapter member lists
- Google Maps for "[trade] contractor [city]" — pull 20–50 per metro, find their owner on LinkedIn

### 5.3 List hygiene (the step everyone skips)

Before sending, run the full list through NeverBounce or ZeroBounce. Drop:
- Invalid (bounces, damages reputation)
- Role-based (info@, sales@, admin@) — often mailing lists, high complaint risk
- Catch-all with unknown verification status (use sparingly — cap at 20% of list)

Your goal: list with <2% bounce rate. The #1 deliverability killer for cold email is sending to bad emails — it's a faster way to kill a domain than any spam trigger word. [[source: Instantly 2026 benchmark report](https://instantly.ai/blog/how-to-achieve-90-cold-email-deliverability-in-2025/)]

### 5.4 Who's actually the decision maker?

For construction subs 10–100 employees, the decision rarely sits with a "CFO." It's typically:

- **<20 employees**: Owner makes the call. Controller/bookkeeper (often part-time) influences.
- **20–50 employees**: Owner + Controller. Owner signs.
- **50–100 employees**: Controller or VP Finance owns evaluation, Owner/CEO signs for anything >$200/mo.

Target two personas per account when possible: the **user** (controller/office manager — feels the pain daily) and the **buyer** (owner — writes the check). Start with the user for inbound emails, but always CC or BCC or mention the owner in proposal stage.

## 6. Cold email — infrastructure, copy, sequences

### 6.1 Deliverability rules you cannot break (2026)

These are hard rules from Google's 2024/2025/2026 enforcement updates and Yahoo/Microsoft matching. Break any one and you lose inbox placement for weeks.

- **SPF, DKIM, and DMARC**: All three, aligned, passing. DMARC minimum `p=none`. [[Google](https://support.google.com/a/answer/81126)]
- **Spam complaint rate**: below 0.30% hard ceiling, target below 0.10%. Above 0.30% = Google stops delivery mitigation for 7+ days.
- **Bounce rate**: below 2%. This is why you verify.
- **Never use your primary domain** for cold outreach. Only secondary domains.
- **Ramp volume slowly**: 5–10 emails/day for first 7 days, 20/day by week 2, 30–50/day steady-state. Never jump.
- **Send volume cap**: 30–50 cold emails per inbox per day, indefinitely. Scale by adding inboxes, not by increasing per-inbox volume.
- **One-click unsubscribe**: RFC 8058 headers + visible footer link on every email. Instantly handles this automatically if enabled.
- **No attachments, no images, no tracking pixels on opens** (in 2026, opens are inflated by Apple MPP to ~45% — they're useless anyway, and tracking pixels can reduce deliverability).

### 6.2 Email copy principles (AR/collections-specific)

- **50–125 words** is the statistically best-performing length. Every word you cut above 125 adds reply probability.
- **Subject lines: 3–5 words, lowercase, no emoji**. Top performers: "quick question," "re: retainage," "[first name] — cash flow."
- **No "{{first_name}}" leakage** — if you don't have the field, don't send. Use a fallback token.
- **No "I help X do Y" openers**. Start with their world, not yours.
- **One ask per email**. Either a reply or a meeting link — never both.
- **No links in email 1**. Links = lower deliverability and more spam-folder placement. Use them in emails 3+.
- **No PDFs, no hosted decks, no tracking images**. Plain text.

### 6.3 The 5-email sequence (construction subcontractor)

Send over 17 days. Email 1 on day 0, then days 3, 7, 12, 17. Weekday sends only (Tue–Thu best). Sending Window: 8–11am prospect local time.

---

**EMAIL 1 — Day 0**
**Subject:** retainage question

```
Hey {{first_name}},

Saw {{company}} on the {{state}} contractor list — {{employee_count}} people, sounds like you're busy.

Quick one: when a GC sits on retainage for 90+ days, what's your follow-up process look like? I'm building something specifically for {{trade}} subs to automate the chase on retainage + final draws without the awkward phone calls.

Worth a 15-min call, or should I just send over what I'm thinking?

— {{sender_first_name}}
```

*Why this works:* Short. One specific pain point (retainage) no generic AR tool handles. Soft CTA. No link. No sales language.

---

**EMAIL 2 — Day 3 (reply to the thread)**
**Subject:** (leave blank — keeps thread)

```
Quick follow-up — I'm probably being presumptuous about retainage being your biggest AR headache.

What's actually eating your time on collections? Final draws? Change orders? Or is it the 60-day invoices from one or two chronically late GCs?

If I'm off base just tell me to go away.

— {{sender_first_name}}
```

*Why this works:* Self-aware. Permission to disqualify. Invites conversation. Typical highest-reply-rate email in a sequence.

---

**EMAIL 3 — Day 7**
**Subject:** (leave blank)

```
{{first_name}} — couple of {{trade}} subs I've talked to this month said the same thing: DSO creeping past 75 days, one AR person who's drowning, QuickBooks reminders that go straight to the GC's spam folder.

If that sounds like you, here's a short Loom (2 min) showing what I'm building: {{loom_link}}

Not asking for a demo yet. Just want to know if this solves something real.

— {{sender_first_name}}
```

*Why this works:* Social proof ("couple of subs this month"). Specific numbers (75 days, one AR person). Loom is low-friction; watching a video doesn't commit to a meeting. First link in the sequence, after relationship warmed up.

---

**EMAIL 4 — Day 12**
**Subject:** (leave blank)

```
Last ask, then I'll stop: do you spend more than 5 hours/week following up on overdue invoices?

If yes and you want to see if this moves the needle, here's 15 min on my calendar: {{cal_link}}

If no, ignore me and I'll close the loop.

— {{sender_first_name}}
```

*Why this works:* Binary question. Direct CTA. Permission to say no.

---

**EMAIL 5 — Day 17 (breakup)**
**Subject:** closing the loop

```
{{first_name}} — I'll stop emailing. If you ever want to compare notes on collections stuff, my DMs are open.

Best of luck on the projects.

— {{sender_first_name}}
```

*Why this works:* The breakup email has consistently high reply rates (~8–12% in published data) because it triggers loss aversion and provides closure. About 20% of your total replies come from email 4 or 5.

### 6.4 Reply handling playbook

**Positive reply ("sure, let's talk")** — reply same day, propose 2 specific time slots in their timezone, include Cal.com link as fallback. Never more than 3 sentences.

**Soft yes / curious ("send more info")** — reply with 2 sentences of relevant specifics plus Loom link plus Cal.com. Don't send a deck. Don't send a brochure. Offer the call.

**Referral ("not me, talk to Jane")** — reply thanking them, then email Jane fresh ("John suggested I reach out re: your AR process — {{rest of email 1}}").

**Objection ("we use Chaser already" / "we're tiny")** — ONE thoughtful reply (use objection scripts in section 9). If they don't re-engage, mark dead. Don't argue.

**Hard no / "remove me"** — add to suppression list immediately. Reply "understood — removed, have a good one." That's it.

**No reply after 5 emails** — mark cold. Re-engage at 90 days with a new angle (launch news, case study, different pain) from a different inbox.

### 6.5 Benchmarks to track weekly

| Metric | Below avg | Avg | Good | Elite |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bounce rate | >3% | 1–2% | <1% | <0.5% |
| Positive reply rate | <1% | 1–3% | 3–5% | 5%+ |
| Total reply rate | <3% | 3–5% | 5–10% | 10%+ |
| Meeting-booked rate (per email sent) | <0.3% | 0.3–0.6% | 0.6–1.2% | 1.2%+ |
| Spam complaint rate | >0.3% = kill list immediately | | <0.1% | ~0% |

Published 2026 B2B SaaS cold email benchmarks: 27.7% average open rate, 3.43% average reply rate (Instantly 2026 report), 5–10% reply rate = good, 10%+ = elite on tight segments. SaaS specifically skews lower — 1.5–3% reply is the category baseline. [[Instantly 2026 benchmark](https://instantly.ai/blog/email-sequence-benchmarks-2026-whats-a-good-open-rate-reply-rate-and-cost-per-meeting/)], [[Cleverly benchmark review](https://www.cleverly.co/blog/cold-email-benchmarks-by-industry)]

If after 500 sends your positive reply rate is below 1.5%, STOP and iterate. The fix is almost always list or email 1 — not sequence length.

### 6.6 Multi-channel cadence (email + LinkedIn + phone)

For your top 50 accounts per week, add channels:

**Day 0**: Email 1 sent.
**Day 1**: LinkedIn profile view (no action — they see you viewed them).
**Day 4**: LinkedIn connection request, no message (40%+ accept rate for SMB owners). Accept ≠ "sell them"; they are now in your network for months.
**Day 7**: Email 3 sends (per sequence).
**Day 8**: LinkedIn message (short, references nothing from email — "saw you've been in roofing for 15 years, wanted to connect"). Never pitch on LinkedIn.
**Day 14**: Phone call if you have a phone number (cell > office). Use a one-line opener: "Hey {{name}}, this is {{you}} — know you're busy, I emailed you last week about collections for {{trade}} subs, got 30 seconds?" Either a yes or a no. Don't overstay.

Do NOT try to run this cadence for every prospect. Run email for everyone, multi-channel only for your top 50–100 accounts/week where the fit is strong.

## 7. Community and inbound pipeline (low-cost, high-compound)

### 7.1 Where to show up

- **r/smallbusiness** (2M+) — daily threads about late payments. Search "get paid" or "client won't pay."
- **r/Construction** and **r/ConstructionManagers** — trade-specific pain.
- **r/QuickBooks** — tactical pain adjacent to your product.
- **Finance Alliance Slack** — vetted finance leaders.
- **CFO Connect Slack** (by Spendesk) — deeper finance audience.
- **AGC online forums / trade association forums** — old-school but active for construction.
- **Contractor Talk** forum — specific to construction.

### 7.2 The 90/10 rule

90% of your activity is giving without asking. Answer 3–5 questions per week substantively — not "you should try my tool," but "here's how I'd structure a 30/60/90 reminder sequence with sample wording." Copy the wording into a comment. Help.

10% is bringing your project up — only when directly asked ("what are you working on?" threads) or when your answer would genuinely include your tool and you disclose clearly.

**Never** DM people who reply to your comments with sales pitches. The community will blacklist you faster than any algorithm.

### 7.3 Convert community activity into pipeline

- Every substantive comment gets a link at the bottom: "more on this: [your DSO calculator]" — the lead magnet.
- Track who DMs you. Don't sell. Ask if they'd do a 15-min call to swap notes.
- Reddit comments stay searchable on Google for years. Your username becomes a credibility signal over months.

### 7.4 Launch platforms (time your launch, don't hit them early)

- **BetaList** — submit at week 2 to start a waitlist (expect 20–150 signups).
- **Product Hunt** — launch in weeks 6–8, when you have 3+ customers to rally as upvoters. Do NOT launch on PH early — a dead launch is worse than no launch.
- **Microlaunch** — submit alongside PH.
- **Hacker News "Show HN"** — post week 6 if you have real technical depth to show.

### 7.5 Expected inbound timeline

Be honest: inbound from content, SEO, and community takes 3–6 months minimum to produce meaningful pipeline. In your first 90 days, expect <20% of your pipeline from inbound. The playbook is cold outreach + community as groundwork, not the reverse.

## 8. Discovery calls — structure, script, qualification

### 8.1 The 30-minute agenda

| Minutes | Phase | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| 0–3 | Set context | Establish agenda, permission to ask questions, mutual time respect |
| 3–18 | Discovery (60% of call) | Uncover current state, pain, impact, decision process |
| 18–22 | Reframe / insight | Share 1–2 things you've seen others do / what they might be missing |
| 22–27 | Propose next step | Pilot structure + price, secure commitment to a specific next action |
| 27–30 | Logistics | Who else is involved, when to follow up, mutual calendar |

Gong.io research consistently shows **top reps let the buyer talk 60–65% of the call**. If you're talking more than 40% of the time, you're losing.

### 8.2 The opening script (word-for-word; memorize)

> "Thanks for making time, {{name}}. Quick plan for the next 30 min — I want to spend most of it understanding your AR process and where it breaks down, then spend a few minutes on what I'm building and whether it could actually help. If it's a fit, I'll propose a next step; if not, I'll tell you so. Sound good?
>
> Before we dig in — what made you pick this call off my email?"

That last question is the single most valuable one in the script. Their answer tells you (a) what in your copy hooked them, (b) what pain is actually top of mind, and (c) which of your messaging angles to lead with. Write down the exact words.

### 8.3 Discovery questions (SPICED adapted for AR / SMB)

Use SPICED (Situation, Pain, Impact, Critical Event, Decision) — it's simpler than MEDDIC and fits sub-$5K ACVs better.

**Situation (minutes 3–7):**
- Walk me through what happens from the moment you send an invoice to when the payment hits the bank. Who's involved?
- How many active GCs or customers are you billing right now? Roughly how many open invoices?
- What tools are in that flow — QuickBooks, spreadsheets, anything else?
- Who owns the "is this invoice paid yet" answer on a given Tuesday?

**Pain (minutes 7–12):**
- Where does the process break down most? Is it a specific customer, a specific step, or just volume?
- What's your DSO look like right now? (If they don't know, that IS the answer — most SMBs don't track it, which is a tell.)
- What's the most frustrating part — for you personally?
- How do you know when an invoice is overdue? Who catches it first?

**Impact (minutes 12–16):** *Quantify. This is where deals are won.*
- How many hours per week goes into following up on late payments? (Across how many people?)
- What percentage of your AR is 60+ days overdue right now?
- If you got that money in 30 days faster, what would you do with it?
- Last time a GC sat on retainage too long — what did that cost you? Walk me through it.

**Critical Event (minutes 16–18):** *Why now?*
- What's changed recently that made you open my email? Is there a specific deal or customer that triggered it?
- If you don't solve this in the next 90 days, what happens?

**Decision (minutes 18–22):**
- If this were a fit, who else would need to weigh in before you'd start a pilot?
- What's worked/not worked when you've added software like this before?
- Have you looked at anything else? What made you not pull the trigger?

### 8.4 Disqualify fast

Reasons to end the call early and politely (and save you both time):
- Under 5 employees, no dedicated AR person, <10 invoices/month — too small, your tool is overkill
- Already using Chaser/Centime and "love it" — not your moment
- Currently going through bankruptcy / major restructuring — wrong time
- Owner is 6 months from selling the business — won't adopt new tools
- Wants a $10/mo tool — price reality check up front saves hours later

Language for graceful disqualification: *"Based on what you're describing, I don't think what I'm building is going to move the needle for you in a meaningful way — and I'd rather tell you that now than waste your time. Happy to keep in touch if things change."* That line has earned me referrals.

### 8.5 Pre-call prep (10 min, every call)

- Pull their company website — read the home page and about page.
- Pull their LinkedIn — note role, tenure, previous companies.
- Check if they have recent news (funding, hires, projects won).
- Run their company name through Google + "lawsuit" or "complaint" — cheap intel on whether they're in a receivables dispute.
- Draft one question specific to their company you'll ask in the first 5 minutes.

## 9. Objection handling — scripts for the 12 objections you'll hear

Use Acknowledge → Clarify → Respond. Never argue. Never rush. Pause after their objection for 2 seconds before responding — silence often makes them elaborate, which gives you what you need.

**1. "It's too expensive."**
> "Makes sense — compared to what, though? Is it more than you'd budgeted, or is it more than the cost of doing nothing? Reason I ask: most of the subs I talk to have 15–20% of AR over 60 days — if that's true for you, $X/month is paying for itself 10x over from the first invoice you collect 30 days faster. But I don't want to assume that's your situation."

**2. "We already use QuickBooks reminders."**
> "Got it. Can I ask — when did you last open QuickBooks and look at the reminder log to see which actually went through and which bounced? In my experience, QB reminders are a start, but they don't handle retainage aging, lien waiver chasing, or escalation to the GC's controller when the PM ghosts you. Would you be open to seeing the difference in 10 minutes?"

**3. "We use Chaser / Centime / BILL already."**
> "Great — those are solid tools. What do you wish they did that they don't? (pause) The reason I ask: most of the {{trade}} subs I talk to who've tried [Chaser] said it handled one-off invoices fine but didn't know what to do with retainage or progress billing, so they still did half the chase manually. If that's you, worth 10 min. If not, I'll stop bothering you."

**4. "We're too small."**
> "How small is too small in your mind? Reason I ask — I'm specifically targeting 10–100 employee subs because that's the range where you're big enough to feel the pain but too small to justify a $1,500/mo enterprise AR tool. The sweet spot."

**5. "We don't have budget."**
> "Fair. When does budget get set — start of your fiscal year, per-project, or owner-discretion? (pause) If it's owner-discretion, the question is really whether the ROI math works. If it takes a quarter to set budget, let's talk in Q1. If the money exists but not earmarked, that's solvable."

**6. "Send me more info."**
> "Happy to — so I don't send a generic deck, what specifically would help? Pricing? A case study? How it handles retainage? And are you the person evaluating, or collecting info for someone else?"

**7. "Call me next quarter."**
> "Understood. What changes next quarter that makes it the right time? (pause) The reason I push — if nothing changes, next quarter looks exactly like this quarter. If something specific is driving the timing, let's put it on the calendar now so I don't drop you." (Then actually book a calendar reminder for Q+1.)

**8. "I need to talk to my partner / accountant / controller."**
> "Makes total sense. Two things — first, what do you think their main concern will be, so I can address it up front? Second, would it help if the three of us got on a 15-min call together? Saves you the telephone-game."

**9. "How do I know this actually works?"**
> "Honest answer: I'm early. I don't have 50 case studies to point to. What I have is a pilot structure — 30 days, $99, I set it up personally, and if it doesn't deliver what we agreed at day 30, you owe me nothing more and you keep the setup. That's how I'm earning trust right now. Does that feel fair?"

**10. "We tried something like this before and it didn't work."**
> "Tell me about that — what specifically didn't work? (listen fully) Yeah, that's a pattern I've seen. What I'd do differently is [specific answer tied to their failure mode — bad integration, too generic, too much automation, etc.]. Want to see if this approach is different enough to be worth a pilot?"

**11. "This feels like something I could build in a spreadsheet."**
> "You could, and some of my prospects have — the question is whether you WILL. If you haven't built the spreadsheet in the last 6 months, you probably won't in the next 6. I'm not selling you something impossible; I'm selling you not having to maintain it."

**12. "I need to think about it." (the silent killer)**
> "Of course. What specifically — is it price, timing, fit, or something else? (pause) I ask because if it's a real no I'd rather know now; if it's 'I need to sleep on it,' I get it. Can I check back Thursday?"

The SaaStr insight worth internalizing: **the #1 objection in SaaS sales is "Not right now."** Nobody needs more software. What they need is an initiative. Part of your job is helping them see why *this* is the initiative for this quarter, not something else.

## 10. Pricing — what to charge, how to present it

### 10.1 Your pricing model (pre-launch)

Three tiers. Flat monthly, no per-user complexity, annual discount = 2 months free (~17%).

| Tier | Monthly | Annual | Who |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pilot** (30 days) | $99 one-time | — | First conversation-to-commitment |
| **Growing** | $149/mo | $1,490/yr | 10–30 employee subs, one AR person |
| **Scaling** | $299/mo | $2,990/yr | 30–100 employee subs, multiple AR/billing staff |
| **Custom** | Contact | — | 100+ employees, multi-entity |

**Why these numbers:** SMB SaaS median entry-level pricing is $15–$29/user/month in 2026, but your value prop is flat-fee (company-wide AR automation), so flat pricing reads more honest. $149/mo lands below the typical "I need to run it by the owner" threshold (~$200/mo) for an SMB owner, which shortens sales cycles dramatically. $299 is priced so the ROI math is trivial — one 30-day-faster invoice on a $50K receivable = 50x return in a single month.

**Why not $49/mo like InvoiceSherpa?** You're not a horizontal tool. You're vertical-specialized with white-glove onboarding. $49/mo prices you below the value you deliver and trains the market to think of you as a commodity.

### 10.2 Presenting price on the call (minute 22–27)

Walk through this in exactly this order:

1. **Anchor the ROI first** — "Based on what you described, you have roughly $X in AR and if we cut DSO by 15 days, that's $Y cash freed up. That's the ROI side."
2. **Then the pilot offer** — "My proposal is a 30-day pilot at $99. I personally onboard you, import your QB data, build your first 3 sequences, and we measure 3 specific outcomes we agree on now. If we hit them, we move to ongoing at $149/mo."
3. **Then the two post-pilot tiers** — present both, describe which fits, let them self-select (most will, because they don't want to feel upsold).
4. **Then silence** — after the number is out of your mouth, shut up. Whoever speaks first loses. This is a cliché because it works.

### 10.3 Annual vs monthly

At this stage, **don't push annual hard**. Monthly is fine — you want retention proof, not prepayment. Offer annual as "2 months free" for anyone who asks, and always offer it at renewal.

### 10.4 "Send me pricing" emails

Always respond with pricing inline, not a link. Hiding price below a "contact us" wall at this ACV drops conversion by 30%+. Respond with:

> "Happy to — for {{company size}}, you're likely looking at $149/mo on our Growing plan ($99 pilot for the first 30 days). Full breakdown: [your pricing page URL]. Worth a 15-min call to confirm fit before you commit even the $99: [Cal.com link]."

## 11. Pilot program — structure, proposal, success criteria

### 11.1 Pilot design (the disciplined version)

Based on the published disciplined-pilot framework (D. Shah et al.), B2B pilots convert 60–90% to paid when structured correctly. Structure matters more than price.

**Six must-haves:**

1. **Fixed end date.** 30 days. Not "until we're sure." Open-ended pilots die.
2. **Three measurable success criteria**, agreed in writing before kickoff. Example: (a) reduce 60+ days past due AR by 20%, (b) automate at least 80% of routine follow-ups, (c) save user 5+ hours/week.
3. **Scheduled check-ins.** Kickoff (day 1), mid-point (day 15), debrief (day 30). All on the calendar before pilot begins.
4. **Clear path to paid.** Explicit: "if we hit 2 of 3 criteria, you move to $149/mo on day 31 by default — no new contract, no second discovery call."
5. **Mutual commitment.** You commit to hands-on setup and weekly responsiveness. They commit to a weekly 15-min check-in and connecting QuickBooks by day 3.
6. **Scope discipline.** No custom features during pilot. If they ask, write it on a "future roadmap" doc and keep the pilot moving.

### 11.2 Pilot proposal template (1 page)

```
PILOT AGREEMENT — {{Company Name}} + {{Your Brand}}
───────────────────────────────────────────
Dates: {{start date}} — {{start date + 30 days}}
Pilot fee: $99 (paid day 0 via Stripe)
Post-pilot monthly rate: $149 (begins day 31 if success criteria met)

THE PROBLEM WE'RE SOLVING
{{1–2 sentences quoting their own language from discovery.}}
e.g. "Invoices over 60 days past due are at 18% of AR. Two people spending
~8 hrs/week on manual collections follow-up. Retainage on 3 major GCs
aging past 90 days with no clear process for escalation."

SUCCESS CRITERIA (we'll measure these on day 30)
1. Reduce 60+ days past due AR by at least 20%.
2. Automate ≥80% of first-touch invoice reminders.
3. Save {{AR person name}} at least 5 hrs/week on routine follow-up.

WHAT I WILL DO
- Day 1: Kickoff call + connect your QuickBooks
- Days 2–5: Build your first 3 reminder sequences (standard, polite-nudge, retainage)
- Days 6–15: Monitor, tune based on GC responses
- Day 15: Mid-pilot check-in
- Days 16–29: Scale to full AR book, tune messaging
- Day 30: Debrief + decision call

WHAT I NEED FROM YOU
- 15-min weekly check-in (Tues 10am, on calendar)
- QuickBooks admin access (read-only is fine)
- Feedback on 1–2 sample emails before first sends go out
- A decision on day 30

IF WE HIT 2 OF 3 CRITERIA
You move to $149/mo on day 31 automatically. Cancel anytime with 30 days notice.

IF WE DON'T
You owe nothing further. Your setup stays active for 30 more days while
you figure out what's next. I get your honest feedback.

Signed digitally (reply "confirmed" to this doc):
{{Your name}} _________ Date _______
{{Their name}} _________ Date _______
```

Google Doc it. They "sign" by replying "confirmed" in email. DocuSign is overkill at $99.

### 11.3 Kickoff call (day 1)

30 minutes. Cover:

1. Confirm success criteria in writing (paste them into the call notes — no ambiguity)
2. Walk through what the next 30 days look like day-by-day
3. Get QuickBooks connected (or schedule it within 48 hours — do not let this slip)
4. Identify the 1–2 chronic late-paying customers to target first (concentrated win > broad rollout)
5. Confirm weekly 15-min check-in is calendared

### 11.4 Weekly check-in structure (15 min)

- What's working (2 min)
- What's not (3 min)
- Metric check — where are we vs success criteria? (5 min)
- Anything I should know about your week (2 min)
- Next week's focus (3 min)

Keep notes in a shared Google Doc so there's a running record.

### 11.5 Day 30 debrief and close

Don't wing this call. Prep:

- One-page metrics summary: before/after for each success criterion, quotes from the AR person, specific dollar impact
- Either "we hit 2 of 3" (you convert) or "we hit 1 of 3, here's what needs to change"

If you hit 2 of 3, the script is:

> "So we hit [criteria]. Based on the agreement, we'd move to $149/mo starting {{date}}. Unless anything's changed on your end, I'll set that up tomorrow — sound good?"

Simple. Direct. Don't re-sell a deal you already won.

If you hit 1 of 3, the script is:

> "Honest conversation — we missed 2 of 3. Here's what I think happened and what I'd change if you wanted to keep going: [specifics]. I'm not going to push you to pay ongoing if it's not ready. Three options: (a) extend the pilot 30 more days at the same $99, (b) move forward on a reduced plan, or (c) part ways and I'll check in Q+1. Your call."

The takeaway close is one of the most powerful moves in early-stage SaaS sales because it reverses the default dynamic. You're telling them the deal might not be right — which makes them want to prove it is.

## 12. CRM, deal tracking, and your sales operating rhythm

### 12.1 Pipeline stages in HubSpot

| Stage | Entry criteria | Exit criteria |
|---|---|---|
| Sourced | On list, verified email | Email 1 sent |
| Contacted | Email 1 sent | Reply received |
| Replied | Any reply (positive/negative) | Meeting booked OR disqualified |
| Meeting Booked | Cal.com booking | Meeting held |
| Discovery Done | Call completed, notes logged | Pilot proposed OR disqualified |
| Pilot Proposed | Proposal sent | Pilot signed OR lost |
| Pilot Active | $99 received, kickoff done | Day 30 decision |
| Won | Moved to paid plan | (closed-won) |
| Lost | No decision / declined | (closed-lost) + reason |

Log every stage transition with a date. Every "Lost" must have a reason: price, fit, competitor, timing, no response, other. Review reasons weekly — they are your highest-value learning data.

### 12.2 Required deal fields

On every deal in HubSpot, capture:
- Vertical / sub-vertical
- Employee count
- Estimated DSO (if captured)
- Tech stack (QuickBooks / Sage / Xero / other)
- Trigger event (why now — filled during discovery)
- Blocker (filled when stuck)
- Expected ARR if won

### 12.3 Daily solo-founder operating rhythm

**8:00–9:00 — Reply loop.** Open Instantly unified inbox and HubSpot. Respond to every reply from yesterday. Move deals through stages.

**9:00–10:00 — LinkedIn + community.** Answer 2–3 questions in communities. Post one LinkedIn update per day (rotating: build-in-public update, customer insight, industry stat, question).

**10:00–12:00 — Deep work.** Build or demo prep or proposal-writing. No email. Phone off.

**12:00–1:00 — Calls block.** Discovery and pilot check-ins stack here.

**1:00–2:00 — Lunch + walk.** Actual lunch. Actual walk. Founders who don't do this burn out at month 3.

**2:00–4:00 — Deep work or more calls.**

**4:00–5:00 — List/prospecting.** Add 100 new prospects to Apollo/list, verify, queue for next day's sends. Tee up tomorrow's campaign.

**5:00–5:30 — Daily close.** Update HubSpot. Note what worked / didn't. Write tomorrow's top 3 priorities.

### 12.4 Weekly review (Friday, 1 hour)

- Funnel metrics: sends → opens → replies → meetings → pilots → paid. Where's the biggest drop?
- Lost-reason review: 3 most common losses this week. Any pattern?
- Copy iteration: one thing to A/B in next week's campaigns.
- List health: bounce rate by domain/inbox.
- Pipeline forecast: what's realistic to close next 2 weeks?
- One question: what's the biggest thing I learned this week that I should change?

## 13. Days 1–90: week-by-week execution

### Week 1 (starting day you turn campaigns on)
- Monday: Campaign goes live. 20 emails × 9 inboxes = 180/day. Respond to every reply same-day.
- Tue–Thu: Same volume. First replies start coming in.
- Friday: Review. If bounce rate >2%, pause and re-verify list.
- **Target**: 900 sends, 20–40 replies, 3–6 meetings booked. Zero pilots yet.

### Week 2
- Monday: Ramp to 30 emails/inbox/day (270/day).
- Tue–Wed: First discovery calls happen. Take notes obsessively.
- Thu: First pilot proposal sent.
- Fri: Review. What phrase in your email 1 is getting replies? What pain is mentioned most?
- **Target**: 1,350 sends cumulative (~2,250 total), 10+ meetings booked, 1 pilot proposal out.

### Week 3
- Ramp to 40 emails/inbox/day (360/day). Volume stops here.
- First pilot signed and kicked off. Day 0 of your first pilot.
- Submit to BetaList for waitlist build.
- **Target**: 1 active pilot, 3+ pilot proposals out.

### Week 4
- Continue sends. Start rotating copy — A/B test email 1 subject line.
- Second pilot signed.
- First community-driven conversation (someone in Reddit or LinkedIn books a call).
- **Target**: 2 active pilots, pipeline of 10+ meetings in the next 2 weeks.

### Weeks 5–6
- First pilot hits day 30 debrief. Hopefully converts to paid ($149/mo).
- Second pilot mid-point check-in.
- Start drafting first case study (even if it's one paragraph — you'll use it everywhere).
- **Target**: 3–5 active pilots, 1–2 converting to paid, MRR $149–$450.

### Weeks 7–8
- Launch on Product Hunt using waitlist + customer upvoters.
- Two more pilots close. First case study published as a LinkedIn post.
- Ask your first converted customer for (a) a G2 review and (b) one referral intro.
- **Target**: 2–3 paying customers, MRR $450–$900.

### Weeks 9–10
- Reach out to bookkeeping firms / CPAs as partners (offer 20% first-year revenue share for referred customers).
- Start writing a second blog post for SEO (first one went up week 2).
- Third pilot converts. Fourth pilot signed.
- **Target**: 3–5 paying customers, MRR $900–$1,500.

### Weeks 11–12
- Begin pushing annual plans to existing customers (2 months free).
- Second vertical test: send 200 emails to staffing agencies. See if the same framework works.
- Reflect, adjust. Your targeting or copy has probably drifted — course-correct.
- **Target**: 5–8 paying customers, MRR $1,500–$3,000, one annual prepay.

### Realistic 90-day outcomes

**Conservative scenario**: 3 paying customers, $450 MRR, zero annual deals.
**Realistic scenario**: 5–8 paying customers, $1,500–$3,000 MRR, 1 annual deal.
**Optimistic scenario**: 10+ paying customers, $3,500+ MRR, 2–3 annual deals.

Solo-founder SaaS benchmarks from interviews of Indie Hackers / Lenny's Newsletter founders: getting to $1K MRR in 90 days is considered strong for a cold-started SaaS with no existing audience. Getting to $3K MRR is considered exceptional. Don't beat yourself up if you land in the conservative range — you're still above the median.

## 14. What to do when things go wrong

### Zero replies in the first 300 sends
99% of the time it's deliverability, not copy. Check in this order:
1. Is email landing in spam? Run [mail-tester.com](https://mail-tester.com) fresh.
2. Is bounce rate above 3%? Re-verify list.
3. Is SPF/DKIM/DMARC still passing? Re-check after any DNS changes.
4. Are you using your primary domain by accident?
5. ONLY THEN — is your copy generic? Rewrite email 1.

### Lots of replies, no meetings booked
Your CTA is the problem. Replace "worth a call?" with something more specific — "worth 15 min on Tuesday?" or share a Loom first and ask for reaction before asking for time.

### Lots of meetings, no pilots
Your discovery isn't uncovering enough pain, or you're jumping to pitch too fast. Record your calls (Fathom) and watch them back. How much are you talking? (Should be <40%.) Are you asking quantified-impact questions? Are you getting to "critical event" — the "why now" trigger?

### Lots of pilots, few convert to paid
Success criteria weren't specific or measurable. Pilots without hard numbers in writing almost always slip into "yeah it was okay, let me think about it." Fix the pilot template first — not the close.

### Churning fast post-pilot
Onboarding is too thin. White-glove onboarding for the first 10 customers is the moat. Once you're 10–15 customers in, build a lightweight self-serve onboarding and keep the white glove for >$299 accounts.

## 15. The five anti-patterns to avoid (common solo-founder traps)

**Anti-pattern 1: Polishing the product before talking to anyone.** You cannot figure out what to build without 20+ discovery calls. Start sending before the product is "ready."

**Anti-pattern 2: Building generic features to be "competitive."** Every hour spent building a feature Chaser already has is an hour not building the retainage / lien-waiver workflow that's your actual moat.

**Anti-pattern 3: Spending $$ on tools you don't need.** Clay, Salesforce, DocuSign, Gong, Outreach — all are $100–$500/mo tools that do nothing for you at 0 customers. The <$200 stack in section 2 is sufficient through your first 30 customers.

**Anti-pattern 4: Free trials instead of paid pilots.** Free trials attract tire-kickers, produce false demand signal, and don't force engagement. A $99 paid pilot filters 10x harder and converts 10x better.

**Anti-pattern 5: Being too patient.** "Let me check back in Q4" is usually the founder losing a deal to politeness. If the pain is real and the prospect is qualified, the call should end with a specific, calendared next step within 14 days — or a clear "no" so you can move on.
